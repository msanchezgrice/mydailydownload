**Date:** June 2025

**Confidence Level:** High (multiple corroborating sources for key findings)

## Executive Summary

The B2B talent marketplace and skill-verified hiring landscape is undergoing a fundamental transformation. Three critical market signals point to a massive opportunity for an employer-facing portal with verified AI skills: (1) LinkedIn discontinued its Skill Assessments program in 2024 because employers wanted contextual skill proof, not multiple-choice badges; (2) the "proof of work" hiring movement is accelerating rapidly; and (3) the candidate skills assessment market is valued at $2.86-$6.3 billion in 2024 and growing at 11-12% CAGR. The gap between skills-based hiring rhetoric and verified AI skill proof at the employer level represents a clear strategic opening.

## 1. Hired — Skill-Vetted Talent Marketplace

### Overview

Hired (originally Vettery, founded 2013 by Brett Adcock and Adam Goldstein) pioneered the "reverse marketplace" model for tech hiring where companies apply to candidates rather than the other way around. [^495^]

**Claim:** Hired operates as a candidate-centric marketplace with salary transparency and skills-based matching.
**Source:** Wikipedia / PR Newswire
**URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hired_(company)
**Date:** 2019-2024
**Excerpt:** "Vettery matches employers with the candidates in its databases, in various fields such as technology, sales, and finance. Companies currently using Vettery to hire employees include Amazon, eBay and JP Morgan."
**Context:** Vettery acquired Hired.com in November 2020, then rebranded the combined entity as Hired in March 2021.
**Confidence:** High

### Key Milestones

- **2013:** Vettery founded in NYC
- **2018:** UK operations launched; acquired by Adecco Group for $100M+ [^495^]
- **2020:** Vettery (via Adecco) acquired Hired.com technology
- **2021:** Unified platform rebranded as Hired, expanding beyond tech to sales roles [^489^]
- **2024:** Hired became part of LHH Recruitment Solutions (Adecco's U.S. professional recruitment brands) [^495^]

### Business Model & Pricing

Hired operates on an employer-pays model with subscription or per-hire fees. While exact current pricing is not publicly disclosed, the marketplace historically charged employers for access to vetted, interview-ready candidates with transparent salary expectations.

**Claim:** Hired offers customized assessments and salary bias alerts to help remove unconscious bias.
**Source:** PR Newswire
**URL:** https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/hired-solidifies-position-as-worlds-largest-ai-driven-hiring-marketplace-amid-record-hiring-activity-301266453.html
**Date:** April 13, 2021
**Excerpt:** "Hired aims to demystify the opaque hiring industry by giving both candidates and employers accurate information, equal access to opportunity, and unparalleled transparency."
**Context:** The platform differentiates through AI-driven matching and DEI-focused features.
**Confidence:** High

### Strategic Significance

Hired's trajectory illustrates both the opportunity and challenge in skill-verified hiring: its reverse marketplace model was genuinely innovative, but its absorption into Adecco's LHH Recruitment Solutions suggests that standalone talent marketplaces struggle to maintain independence without broader HR service ecosystems. The platform's core insight — that candidates with transparent salary data and verified skills create more efficient matching — remains highly relevant.

## 3. Triplebyte — What Happened and Why It Matters

### Shutdown Timeline

Triplebyte was once the most promising skills-first hiring platform, but its shutdown provides crucial lessons for anyone building in this space.

**Claim:** Triplebyte was acquired by Karat in March 2023 and completely shut down by March 31, 2023.
**Source:** Karat / Hacker News
**URL:** https://connect.karat.com/tb-candidates
**Date:** March 2023
**Excerpt:** "On March 16, 2023, Karat announced the acquisition of Triplebyte's technical assessment product and team. At the same time, Triplebyte shared that they would be winding down their services effective March 31st... Triplebyte's services - including their candidate profiles and sourcing products - have been shuttered and have not been transitioned over to Karat."
**Context:** Candidates lost access to all profiles and assessment scores with less than 2 weeks notice.
**Confidence:** High

### Why It Failed: The Founder's Own Words

**Claim:** Triplebyte's founder admitted they were "never able to make the marketplace version of Triplebyte work after moving away from the transactional model."
**Source:** Hacker News (Triplebyte founder comment)
**URL:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=35184546
**Date:** March 16, 2023
**Excerpt:** "We were simply never able to make the marketplace version of Triplebyte work after moving away from the transactional model we had in the early days. We tried some new tactics near the end that seemed to be bearing fruit, and I think in a world where tech hadn't crashed in mid to late 2022 we would have had a reasonable shot."
**Context:** The tech downturn of 2022 was the immediate cause, but structural business model issues were the root cause.
**Confidence:** High

### Critical Lessons from Triplebyte's Failure

1. **The marketplace chicken-and-egg problem is brutal**: Need both employers and candidates at scale simultaneously
2. **HR departments resist giving up power**: "No HR org wants to hand over a huge chunk of their power and purpose within a company" (Hacker News comment)
3. **The VC growth model doesn't fit hiring**: "Their business model also didn't fit a VC startup model, no real room for exponential revenue growth" (Hacker News comment)
4. **Scaling high-quality assessment is hard**: "How can you have a hundred Ammons interviewing and assessing people? It just doesn't scale" (referring to founder Ammon Bartram's personal interview quality)
5. **Signal erosion**: Standards slid over time as the platform pursued growth, diluting the value of passing

### Impact Stories

Despite its failure, Triplebyte had genuine impact:
- A construction worker became a senior engineer at a major SV company earning $200K+
- A former Amazon warehouse packer of 10 years landed a $200K+ engineering job
- Multiple hiring managers integrated Triplebyte into their hiring process for reliable skill ranking

**Confidence:** High

## 4. LinkedIn Skills Assessments — Discontinued in 2024

### The Most Important Finding in This Report

**LinkedIn discontinued its Skill Assessments feature and removed all associated badges from member profiles in 2024.** This is a seismic event in the skill-verification space that creates a massive market gap.

**Claim:** LinkedIn discontinued Skill Assessments in 2024 because "hirers said that examples of how a candidate applied their skills is increasingly valuable to assess fit."
**Source:** MarkHub24 (citing LinkedIn Help Center)
**URL:** https://www.markhub24.com/post/linkedin-skill-badges-as-a-professional-branding-tool
**Date:** March 13, 2026
**Excerpt:** "LinkedIn discontinued the Skill Assessments feature and removed all associated badges from member profiles in 2024. The official rationale, as documented in LinkedIn's own Help Center announcement, was: 'We've heard from hirers that examples of how a candidate applied their skills is increasingly valuable to assess fit.'"
**Context:** LinkedIn shifted strategy to allow members to tag skills to specific credentials, jobs, projects, or education entries — contextual skill demonstration over abstract testing.
**Confidence:** Very High

### Why This Matters

LinkedIn's Skill Assessments represented the largest-scale attempt at platform-verified professional skills. Their failure reveals critical insights:

1. **Multiple-choice tests don't predict job performance**: "A standardized multiple-choice test can assess recall and conceptual understanding — but professional performance involves contextual judgment, collaboration, iterative problem-solving, and applied creativity that no 15-question adaptive quiz can capture"

2. **Recruiter trust is fragile**: "A badge ecosystem requires a sustained platform-side investment in recruiter education and trust-building that LinkedIn appears not to have sufficiently maintained"

3. **The "get hired" outcome didn't materialize**: "Once hirer confidence declined, the entire flywheel lost its terminal incentive"

4. **Context > Certification**: LinkedIn's pivot to "contextual skill tagging" validates that employers want to see *how* skills were applied, not just that they exist

### Market Data from LinkedIn's Era

- 80% of hiring managers had encountered candidates who did not have the skills they claimed [^453^]
- Early data showed 30% improvement in hire likelihood with Skill Assessments
- LinkedIn's Talent Solutions surpassed $7B in revenue in FY2023
- LinkedIn's total revenue surpassed $15B in FY2023
- By 2023, 26% of paid job posts did not require a degree (vs. 22% in 2020)

**Confidence:** Very High

## 9. The Critical Gap: Where Verified AI Skill Proof Fits

### The Problem Employers Face

Employers are caught in a perfect storm:

1. **AI skills are in massive demand but hard to verify**: "The vast majority of companies plan to invest significantly in AI over the next decade, while Forbes reported in 2024 that employers would rather hire a less experienced candidate with AI literacy than a more experienced candidate without it" [^479^]

2. **Traditional credentials don't exist for AI skills**: Most AI skills (prompt engineering, AI workflow design, human-AI collaboration) are too new for degree programs or standardized tests

3. **Self-reported skills are unreliable**: "75% of global knowledge workers used GenAI at work" (Microsoft/LinkedIn, May 2024) — but usage doesn't equal proficiency

4. **Assessment fatigue**: Candidates face dozens of one-off assessments per job application

5. **AI-generated resume inflation**: "91% of hiring managers have caught candidates misrepresenting skills through AI tools" [^559^]

### The Opportunity: A Portable, Verified AI Skills Portfolio

The gap that an employer-facing portal with verified AI skills could fill:

**What employers want (based on discontinuation of LinkedIn Skill Assessments):**
- Contextual evidence: how skills were applied, not just that they exist
- Work samples: projects, outputs, artifacts
- Third-party verification: not self-reported
- Currency: skills verified recently, not years ago
- Portability: works across platforms, follows the candidate

**What the market lacks:**
- No platform focuses specifically on AI skill verification with work samples
- No platform provides a unified AI skills portfolio that employers trust
- CodeSignal/HackerRank verify coding skills but not AI-specific competencies
- LinkedIn abandoned skill assessments entirely
- Turing/Toptal verify developers but not general AI literacy

## 10. Key Risks & Cautions

### Risks Identified in Research

1. **Platform trust is hard to build, easy to lose**: LinkedIn's experience shows that even with massive resources, skill verification signals can erode

2. **The "chicken and egg" marketplace problem**: Triplebyte's failure demonstrates how hard it is to build both sides simultaneously

3. **HR department resistance**: Triplebyte's HN commenters noted HR orgs resist ceding control over hiring processes

4. **Skills obsolescence rate**: The "half-life" of digital skills is now ~4 years, and AI-specific skills just 2.5 years [^455^] — verification must be continuously refreshed

5. **AI-assisted cheating**: "Over half of developers admit to using AI in coding tests" [^486^] — verification systems must be AI-resistant

6. **Scale vs. quality tradeoff**: Turing's fully automated vetting enables scale but creates occasional mismatches; Toptal's human vetting limits supply

7. **Badge inflation**: As more platforms offer "verified" skills, the signal value of any single badge decreases

## 11. Strategic Recommendations

### For an Employer-Facing AI Skills Portal

Based on this research, the following strategic positioning would maximize success:

1. **Focus on "proof of work" not "proof of test"**: Show how AI skills were applied in real projects, not just MCQ or coding test scores. This aligns with the #1 lesson from LinkedIn's Skill Assessment discontinuation.

2. **Integrate with Workday/Degreed ecosystem**: The enterprise skills infrastructure is consolidating around Workday Skills Cloud and Degreed integrations. Being part of this ecosystem is critical for B2B adoption.

3. **Target the verification gap**: Position as the solution to "AI resume inflation" — provide third-party verified evidence that candidates actually possess claimed AI skills.

4. **Build employer-side value first**: The Triplebyte lesson is clear — without employer demand, the candidate side doesn't matter. Start with employers who need verified AI talent.

5. **Emphasize currency and recency**: Given AI skill half-life of 2.5 years, verification must demonstrate recent application, not just past learning.

6. **Offer portfolio + assessment hybrid**: Combine Fueler's portfolio model with CodeSignal's rigorous assessment — contextual work samples plus standardized evaluation.

7. **Price for enterprise, not consumers**: The B2B market (assessment platforms $2.86-6.3B) is vastly larger than B2C credentialing. CodeSignal charges $19K+ for enterprise; HackerRank at $165/mo for starter tiers.
